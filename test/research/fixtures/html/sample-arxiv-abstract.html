Reciprocal Rank Fusion for Heterogeneous Personal Corpora

A. Researcher, B. Coauthor, C. Lastauthor

Submitted on 15 Dec 2025

Abstract

Personal-scale document collections present a challenging environment for retrieval systems. Unlike web-scale corpora, where average behavior dominates and outliers can be ignored, personal corpora are dominated by long tails of idiosyncratic terminology, meeting notes, code snippets, and casual writing. We argue that this heterogeneity makes single-strategy retrieval — whether lexical or dense — systematically worse than a fused approach that combines the strengths of both.

In this paper we evaluate reciprocal rank fusion (RRF) as a practical baseline for personal retrieval and compare it against learned-fusion alternatives across a range of corpus sizes and document distributions. We find that RRF is remarkably robust: it matches or beats more sophisticated fusion methods on every corpus we tested, while requiring no per-corpus training or hyperparameter tuning. The simplicity is significant: it means an end-user product can ship RRF with confidence, knowing that the user's corpus does not have to look like the training distribution for retrieval to work well.

We also study failure modes. The most consistent failure of RRF on personal corpora occurs when both retrievers retrieve the same wrong document near the top of their ranked lists, an effect we call correlated false positive. We propose a simple diversification step that detects this case using a learned confidence score on the top-k overlap and falls back to a more conservative ranking when correlation is high. This change adds less than a millisecond of latency per query but eliminates the majority of correlated-false-positive errors in our benchmark.

Our experiments use four publicly released personal-corpus analogs spanning roughly 5,000 to 200,000 documents, and we report NDCG@10, MRR, and a new "user-actionable" metric that measures how often the ground-truth document appears in the top three results. Across all four corpora, our diversified-RRF approach improves user-actionable hits by between 6% and 11% relative to plain RRF, with no statistically significant change in NDCG@10. We release the code and the benchmark as supplementary material.

1. Introduction

Information retrieval at internet scale has been studied for decades, but personal-scale retrieval — the problem of searching one user's documents on one user's machine — has received much less attention. Yet many emerging product categories, including local-first knowledge tools and on-device AI assistants, depend critically on solving this problem. The constraints are different from those of web search in ways that matter for ranking design.
